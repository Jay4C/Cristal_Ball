Energy of a magnetic moment under magnetic field
The potential energy of a magnet of magnetic moment, in a magnetic field, is defined as the mechanical work of the magnetic force (in fact, torque magnetic) on the re-alignment of the vector of the magnetic moment dipole, and is equal to :
\[ E_{p,m} = - \vec{m} * \vec{B} \]

Energy stored in an inductor
Consider an ideal inductor (of L inductor). In order to pass a current through the inductor, it is necessary to bring energy to start the current. This energy, which is then stored by the magnetic field of the inductor, is recoverable once the current is turned off.
\[ P = U * I = L * I * \frac{\mathrm d I}{\mathrm d t} \]
\[ E_{stored} = \int_{0}^{t} P dt = \int_{0}^{I} L * I^{'} dI^{'} = \frac{L * I^{2}}{2} \]
\[ B = \frac{\mu * N * I}{l} \]
\[ L = \frac{\mu * N^{2} * A}{l} \]
\[ u = \frac{Energy}{volume} = \frac{\frac{L * I^{2}}{2}}{A * l} = \frac{B^{2}}{2 * \mu_{0}} \]
\[ E = \frac{1}{2} * \int \vec{H} * \vec{B} dV \]

Magnetic flux
The magnetic flux or magnetic induction flux, is a measurable physical quantity characterizing the intensity and spatial distribution of the magnetic field. This quantity is equal to the flux of the magnetic induction through an oriented surface. This flow is by definition the scalar product of these two vectors1 (see mathematical definition below). Its unit of expression in the International System of Units is the weber (unit homogeneous with volts-seconds).
\[ d\Phi = \vec{B} * d\vec{S} = \lVert \vec{B} \rVert * \lVert d\vec{S} \rVert * cos(\theta) \]
\[ \Phi = \iint\limits_S d\Phi = \iint\limits_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{S} \]
\[ \vec{B} = \vec{rot(\vec{A})} \]
\[ \Phi_{B} = \int\limits_s \vec{A} \cdot \vec{dl} \]
\[ e(t) = - \frac{\mathrm d \Phi(t)}{\mathrm d t} \]

Electromagnetic induction
Electromagnetic induction is a physical phenomenon leading to the appearance of an electromotive force in an electrical conductor subjected to a variable magnetic field flux. This electromotive force can generate an electric current in the conductor. This phenomenon is of paramount practical importance. It is used in particular in generators and electrical transformers, coils, or even induction plates thanks to eddy currents.
\[ \vec{F} = q * \vec{E} + q * \vec{v} \times \vec{B} \]
\[ e = \oint (\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \cdot \vec{dl} \]
\[ \vec{j} = \sigma * (- \vec{\nabla V} + \vec{E_{em}}) \]
\[ \vec{E_{em}} = -\frac{\partial \vec{A}}{\partial t} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B} \]
\[ \epsilon = -\frac{\mathrm d\Phi}{\mathrm d t} \]
\[ U = R * I - \epsilon \]
\[ \epsilon = -\frac{\mathrm d\phi}{\mathrm d t} \]
\[ u = - L * \frac{\mathrm d i}{\mathrm d t} \]

Induced current
The induced current is an electric current which, by its effects, opposes the causes which gave rise to it (Lenz's law).
\[ \epsilon = -\frac{\mathrm d\Phi}{\mathrm d t} \]
\[ U = - N * \frac{\mathrm d\Phi}{\mathrm d t} \]

Electromotive force
The electromotive force, or electromotance1, is one of the characteristic parameters of an electric generator. It is, contrary to what its name indicates, homogeneous with a voltage and is expressed in volts. In an electric circuit, to set loads in motion, it is necessary to supply work, and the electromotive force corresponds to the work which a generator supplies to the circuit per unit of load. This force is most often provided by an electric generator, which imposes an electric voltage across its terminals. In this case, the electromotive force is associated with a point component. In the case of electromagnetic induction, the electromotive force often takes the form of a counter-electromotive force, opposing in its opposite direction the establishment of a magnetic field. In such a case, it is distributed over the entire circuit, the electromotive force is associated with a mesh of the circuit, and results from the curvilinear integral on this mesh of the electromagnetic force induced on each element of length by the variation of the magnetic field.
\[ e = \frac{W}{i * t} \]
\[ \vec{rot(\vec{E})} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \]
\[ e = \oint_C \vec{E} \cdot \vec{dl} = - \frac{\mathrm d\phi}{\mathrm dt} \]
\[ dG = - S * dT + V * dP + e * dq \]
\[ (\frac{\partial e}{\partial T})_{q} = (\frac{\partial S}{\partial q})_{T} \]

Lenz–Faraday law
An electrical circuit, represented by an arbitrarily oriented contour C, subjected to a variable magnetic flux Φ (resulting from a variable magnetic field B) is the seat of an electromotive force e such that:
\[ e = - \frac{\mathrm d\Phi}{\mathrm dt} \]
\[ e = \oint \vec{E} \cdot \vec{dl} \]
\[ \Phi = \iint_S \vec{B} \cdot \vec{n} * dS \]
\[ \oint \vec{E} \cdot \vec{dl} = - \iint_S \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \cdot \vec{n} * dS \]
\[ \vec{rot(\vec{E})} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \]
\[ \Phi = \iint_\Sigma \vec{B} \cdot \vec{n} * d\Sigma = \iint_\Sigma (\vec{rot(\vec{A})}) \cdot \vec{n} * d\Sigma = \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot \vec{dl} \]

Maxwell's equations
Maxwell's equations, also called Maxwell-Lorentz equations, are fundamental laws of physics. They constitute, with the expression of the electromagnetic force of Lorentz, the basic postulates of electromagnetism.
\[ div(\vec{E}) = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \]
\[ \oint_\Sigma \vec{E} \cdot \vec{dS} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} * \int_V \rho * d\tau = \frac{Q_{int}}{\epsilon_0} \]
\[ \oint_\Sigma \vec{B} \cdot \vec{dS} = 0 \]
\[ div(\vec{B}) = 0 \]
\[ \vec{B} = \vec{rot(\vec{A})} \]
\[ \vec{rot(\vec{E})} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \]
\[ e = - \frac{\mathrm d \Phi}{\mathrm dt} \]
\[ e = \oint_C \vec{E} \cdot \vec{dl} \]
\[ \Phi = \int_S \vec{B} \cdot \vec{dS} \]
\[ \vec{rot(\vec{grad(F)})} = 0 \]
\[ \vec{E} = - \vec{grad(V)} - \frac{\partial \vec{A}}{\partial t} \]
\[ \vec{rot(\vec{B})} = \mu_0 * \vec{j} + \mu_0 * \epsilon_0 * \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \]
\[ \oint_C \vec{B} \cdot \vec{dl} = \mu_0 * \int_S (\vec{j} \cdot \vec{dS}) + \mu_0 * \epsilon_0 * \int_S (\frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \cdot \vec{dS}) \]
\[ div(\vec{j}) + \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} * \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0 \]
\[ - \nabla^{2}\vec{E} + \mu_0 * \epsilon_0 * \frac{\partial^{2} \vec{E}}{\partial t^{2}} = - \vec{grad(\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0})} - \mu_0 * \frac{\partial \vec{j}}{\partial t} \]
\[ - \nabla^{2}\vec{B} + \mu_0 * \epsilon_0 * \frac{\partial^{2} \vec{B}}{\partial t^{2}} = - \vec{rot(\mu_0 * \vec{j})} \]
\[ c^{2} = \frac{1}{\mu_0 * \epsilon_0} \]
\[ \vec{B} = \vec{rot(\vec{A})} \]
\[ \vec{E} = - \vec{grad(V)} - \frac{\partial \vec{A}}{\partial t} \]
\[ \frac{1}{c^{2}} * \frac{\partial^{2} \vec{A}}{\partial t^{2}} - \nabla^{2}\vec{A} = \mu_0 * \vec{j} \]
\[ \frac{1}{c^{2}} * \frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial t^{2}} - \nabla^{2}V = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \]

Poynting's theorem
Poynting's theorem, stated by John Henry Poynting, concerns the conservation of energy in an electromagnetic field. It establishes a relation between electromagnetic energy, Joule effect and the flux of the Poynting vector. In informal terms, the flux of the Poynting vector through a closed surface can be said to be equal to the sum of the change in electromagnetic energy and the Joule effect in the interior volume at the surface.
\[ - \iiint \frac{\partial W_{em}}{\partial t} * d\tau = \iiint div(\vec{\Pi}) * d\tau + \iiint \vec{j} \cdot \vec{E} * d\tau \]
\[ - \frac{\partial (\frac{\epsilon_0 * E^{2}}{2} + \frac{B^{2}}{2 * \mu_0})}{\partial t} = div(\frac{\vec{E} \times \vec{B}}{\mu_0}) + \vec{j} \cdot \vec{E} \]
\[ - \frac{\partial (\frac{\vec{E} \cdot \vec{D}}{2} + \frac{\vec{B} \cdot \vec{H}}{2})}{\partial t} = div(\vec{E} \times \vec{H}) + \vec{j} \cdot \vec{E} \]

Poynting Vector
In physics, the Poynting vector is the flux density related to the propagation of the electromagnetic wave. Its direction is the direction of propagation. The flux of the Poynting vector through a surface (closed or not) is equal to the power carried by the wave through this surface. The modulus of this vector is therefore a power per unit area, that is to say an energy flux density; it is homogeneous with an energetic illumination and an energetic exitance; and, in the International System (SI) of units, it is expressed in watts per square meter.
\[ \frac{\partial e(t)}{\partial t} + div(\vec{\Pi(t)}) = s(t) \]
\[ \vec{\Pi(t)} = \frac{\vec{E(t)} \times \vec{B(t)}}{\mu_0} \]
\[ \vec{\Pi(t)} = \vec{E(t)} \times \vec{H(t)} \]
\[ P_{S} = \iint_S (\vec{\Pi(t)} \cdot \vec{dS}) \]
\[ U_{em} = \iiint_V (W_{em} * d\tau) \]
\[ - \frac{\partial W_{em}}{\partial t} = div(\vec{P}) + \vec{j} \cdot \vec{E} \]

Magnetization
Magnetization, usually denoted by the symbol M (in capitals), is defined as the volume density of magnetic moment.
\[ M = \frac{\mathrm dm}{\mathrm dV} \]
M = n * mean_value(m)

Magnetic susceptibility
The magnetic susceptibility of a material seen as a continuous medium is a dimensionless quantity which characterizes the ability of this material to become magnetized under the effect of a magnetic excitation. Any material, if it can be seen as a continuous and homogeneous medium, has a homogeneous magnetic field which is written in the form :
\[ \vec{B} = \mu_0 * (\vec{H} + \vec{M}) \]
B, H, M represent vector fields whose norms are: H for the applied or external magnetic field, also called magnetic excitation, that is to say the field that exists in the absence of the material. It is expressed in amperes per meter (A/m). M for the magnetic response of the material when the latter is placed in the presence of H, this response is called "magnetization" and is also expressed in amperes per meter (A/m), mu_0 is the permeability of the vacuum, it allows the conversion of H+M in B which do not have the same units of measurement. B for the total field inside the material, it is called magnetic induction and is expressed in tesla (T).
\[ \vec{B} = \mu * \vec{H} \]
