Electrostatic potential energy of particule
The electrostatic potential energy (or simply electrostatic energy) of an electric charge q placed at a point P bathed in an electric potential V(P) is defined as the work to be provided to transport this charge from infinity to the position P.

We have :
\[ E_{potential, particule} = q * V(P) \]

Kinetic energy of particule
Kinetic energy is related to the movement of the particle.

We have :
\[ E_{kinetic, particule} = \frac{ m * v^{2} }{ 2 } \]
\[ E_{kinetic, particule} = \frac{ p^{2} }{ 2 * m } \]
\[ E_{kinetic, particule} = \frac{ h^{2} }{ \lambda^{2} * 2 * m } \]

Mechanical energy of particule
We have :
\[ E_{mechanical, particule} = \frac{ m * v^{2} }{ 2 } + q * V(P) \]
\[ E_{mechanical, particule} = \frac{ p^{2} }{ 2 * m } + q * V(P) \]
\[ E_{mechanical, particule} = \frac{ h^{2} }{ \lambda^{2} * 2 * m } + q * V(P) \]

Photonic energy
The energy of photons is the energy carried by a single photon. The amount of energy is directly proportional to the electromagnetic frequency of the photon and therefore, equivalently, is inversely proportional to the wavelength. The higher the frequency of the photon, the higher its energy. Equivalently, the longer the wavelength of the photon, the lower its energy.

We have :
\[ E_{photon} = \frac{h * c}{\lambda} \]
\[ E_{photon} = h * f \]
\[ E_{photon} = h * \nu \]
\[ E_{photon} = \frac{h * \omega}{2 * \pi} \]

Paschen's law
Paschen's law, an empirical law enunciated by the German physicist Friedrich Paschen in 1889, indicates that the appearance of an electric arc in a gas, at a certain electric breakdown field (called disruptive field), is a generally nonlinear function of the product of the pressure p of the gas by the distance d between the electrodes.

Paschen's law states that the breakdown voltage of a gas subjected to a continuous potential difference V, between electrodes separated by a distance d, obeys an equation of the form:
\[ V_{breakdown} = \frac{ B * p * d }{ C + ln(p * d) } \]
where B and C are characteristic constants of the electrodes and the gas and where p is the pressure of the gas. The curves giving the graphical representations of V_breakdown as a function of the product p * d are called Paschen curves.

We have :
\[ V_{breakdown} = \frac{ B * p * d }{ ln(A) - ln[ ln(1 + \frac{ 1 }{ \gamma }) ] + ln(p * d) } \]
where A is a characteristic of the gas and gamma depends of the nature of the electrodes.
